In a Changing New York, Evictions are on the Rise

By: Mary Beech

December 22, 2025

In less than 10 days’ time, New York City will inaugurate its youngest mayor in over a century - a democratic socialist who, mere weeks before this year’s primary, was considered a long shot candidate. And while pundits may not have predicted his win, they tend to agree that he achieved it running first and foremost on a platform of affordability for all New Yorkers.

One of Mamdani's flasghip issues? "Freeze the Rent." But why did it resonate to such a degree with the city's voters?

New York is in the throes of a historic housing shortage, with the rental vacancy rate at 1.4% - the lowest it has been since 1968 - according to the Department of Housing Preservation and Development. Late last year, City Council passed a monumental rezoning initiative - dubbed “City of Yes” - to spur new development. Even so, a 2024 analysis by McKinsey & Company estimates that the city would have to return to levels of housing production unseen since the 1950s in order to close what they estimate is a roughly 540,000 unit shortfall.

And while rental units are widely hard to find, they are also historically unaffordable: the Comptroller's office reports that the median "asking rent" on publicly-listed apartments has been at a record high since 2023, at $3,500 per month. And as rents - and the household incomes required to afford them - trend upward, so do eviction rates, according to a new analysis by the New York City News Service.

By cross-referencing publicly available New York City eviction data with household-income data from the United States Census Bureau, reporters found that when the median household income in a single neighborhood increased, so did the number of eviction filings in that neighborhood. In other words, as a neighborhood gentrifies - as wealthier renters move in and poorer ones move out - eviction filings in that neighborhood tend to go up.

The analysis isolated the ten New York City zip codes with the highest median household income change over a 4-year period. In every single one of those zip codes, evictions rose along with median household income.
